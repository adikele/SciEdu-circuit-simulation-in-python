Objective of this assignment is to illustrate two basic signal processing functions and clarify their relationship to physics and mathematics.

Background

In real communication systems we often need to carry out basic mathematical operations with two signals, we add and multiply them with each other. Here we illustrate main differences between these processes. Multiplication we need, for example, in Continuous wave (CW) modulation to transfer signal to another frequency band. Adder cannot be used for this, as we will see below.

Sum of Two Signals

Adding two signals is a linear process, which means that no new frequency component appear when two signals are added together. For example output of an ideal linear amplifier that has voltage gain g, becomes

uout = g uin

If input signal uin is a sum of two signals, here two cosine waveforms at frequencies f1 and f2 output becomes

g (A1cos(2πf1t) + A1cos(2πf1t)) = gA1cos(2πf1t) + gA1cos(2πf1t)

Output signal consists of the same frequencies than input signal. No new frequency components are created. Both signals remain on their original frequency bands and no frequency transfer takes place. For this we need multiplication process.

Product of Two Signals

Multiplication is a nonlinear process and it creates new frequency components while original frequencies disappear. For simplicity we use here two cosine waveforms oscillating at different frequencies. A basic trigonometric formula that explains what actually happens is

cos⁡α * cos⁡β = 1/2 cos⁡(α-β)+ 1/2 cos⁡(α+β)

When we have two physical cosine waveforms at frequencies f1 and f2, and amplitudes A1 and A2, we write

A1 cos (2πf1t) * A2 cos (2πf2t) = (A1 A2)/2 cos (2π(f2 - f1 )t) + (A1 A2)/2 cos (2π(f2 + f1 )t)

We see that original frequencies disappear and the new ones, sum and difference frequencies are produced by multiplication process.

Multiplication is the key process in Continuous Wave (CW) modulation, where high frequency (f2) carrier wave is multiplied by a low frequency (f1) message (representing information that we want to transmit), spectrum of the message, i.e. f1, is transferred to both sides of the carrier wave. Then multiplication of two frequencies f1 and f2 produces two new frequencies f2 + f1, and f2 - f1. These are called upper and lower sidebands.

Note that cos (α - β) = cos (β - α), i.e. cosine waveform at frequency at frequency f1 - f2 is the same as at frequency f2 - f1.
